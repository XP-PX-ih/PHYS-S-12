3D Printing

For 3D printing, I did a test run of a chassis style for my final project. The 3D modeling of the chassis was not too difficult, looking more complex than it really is, with me mostly just using the loft and fillet tools. I split the base from the top so I could open it and add the electrical components later. The screw holes are sized for M3 screws, and I added small hexagonal holes for M3 lock nuts to go as well. In total, the chassis itself has 10 screws.

[Insert Images from Fusion]

I first printed a small version for lab and to make sure the shape worked. The first print failed as I had selected the wrong plastic type in Prusa. After changing this, it worked rather well.

I then started the prints of a full scale model. The main purpose of this print was to test form factor and screw fittings. The top did not fully finish printing because I did not check if there was enough filament when I left for the day. However, from what I have it is clear that the mouse is too big. I found that I needed to increase the diameter of area for the nuts, as the extra width from the 3D printer prevented it from fitting in. Otherwise, the screws fit nicely. For the final product, I will add holes for the buttons and make major tweaks to the form factor.

3D Scanning

I originally intended to 3D scan a clay model of the mouse chassis. This would have allowed me to grip the mouse and know if the form factor was acceptal before printing a smoother version. However, I decided against it as my modeling skills simply have too many imperfections that would result in me spending lots of time fixing them in Blender.

I instead worked with Viraj again and we decided to scan our heads. It took around 30 minutes for Viraj to scan my head and merge all the parts correctly, and it took me about the same time, a little more due to processing errors. We had to change the scan setting to "Dark" and increase the scan distance for the scanner to actually work.

Final Project Information

Chosen Final Project: One Handed Keyboard+Mouse

The design of the mouse chassis is derived from a previous experiment on mouse shapes done by Dan Odell and Peter Johnson, especially their Model H. In place of the usual mouse buttons are eight keyboard keys that follow the ARTSEY.IO layout. The actual mouse buttons are located on the side for the thumb to click. In the same area is a scroll wheel attached horizontally to the base. The top separates from the bottom. The base is symmetrical across the horizontal axis, while the top piece is asymmetrical and must be switched to accomadate a different dominant hand.

Dimensions:

Length: 150mm; Width: 60mm; Height: 60mm

Side Slope Angle: 20 degree Angle of Depression


Project Goals:

1. Allow for simultaneous command of keyboard and mouse with one hand.

2. Modularity: Top chassis can be swapped to allow for both right and left handed use.

3. Allow for typing speeds of at least 40 word per minute with one hand.

4. Enable the mouse to be detected as a USB or Wireless device in Windows 11.
